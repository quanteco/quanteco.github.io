On the Application of Multiple Species Approaches to Estimate Survival in Cormack Jolly Seber Capture-Recapture Models

Gregory B. Anderson

Understanding the survival process of wild populations can be critical in managing and conserving animal populations due to the strong impact it has on population dynamics. Within the capture-recapture literature, the Cormack Jolly Seber Model is often used because of its universal approach to estimating apparent survival while accounting for imperfect capture and the few assumptions that are made in order to estimate these parameters. In this paper, I focus on the extension of the multi-species approach to the estimation of survival parameters within a hierarchical modeling format. In doing so, I pull on two separate modeling strategies developed for multi-species demographic inference: (1) community modeling occupancy estimation procedures and (2) models of synchrony developed for multinomial mark-resight models. I also demonstrate the use of additional relationships between the species within the sampling inference that can take the form of guilds, families, or even biological or ecological traits.

Do Models that Account for Imperfect Detection Outperform Logistic Regression or Classification Trees for Species Distribution Models?

Gregory B. Anderson

Correctly modeling a species distribution or range can be important in many management and conservation efforts, and occurrence is a summary of habitat suitability (Boyce and McDonald 1999). However, most SDM have largely overlooked a real problem in the observation process: that detectability (p) of most species is imperfect (i.e., a species will not always be detected when it occurs at a site; MaKenzie et al. 2002, 2006; Tyre et al. 2003). Consequently, most studies do not actually model the true occurrence of a species, and if detection is less than one, species distributions will be underestimated. Although several studies have examined the importance of estimating detection probability when using presence-absence data, these studies have not focused on the effect imperfect detection has on out-of-sample predictability. While true absences are hard to observe within real wildlife populations, simulations provide an approach where site occupancy status can be known perfectly. The objective of this study was to assess the importance of accounting for imperfect detection within SDM from an out-of-sample prediction perspective. Several scenarios were simulated to examine how classification trees, logistic regression, and site-occupancy models performed in independent testing data sets.

Using Landscape Genetics to Quantify Genetic Fragmentation and Connectivity Within and Between Aquatic Habitats

Gregory B. Anderson

The recently emerged field of landscape genetics provides new analytical methods to relate the genetic characteristics of populations to landscape features that possibly restrict or facilitate gene flow. Although landscape genetic methods have been primarily used within terrestrial systems, the field has also expanded into freshwater and marine systems. Aquatic systems are of particular interest, since the networks can be easily disrupted, altering the gene flow of populations. Also of interest, are complex multi-dimensional systems that can depend on both terrestrial and aquatic connectivity. In an effort to give an overview of the application of landscape genetics to quantify genetic fragmentation and connectivity within and between aquatic habitats, I reviewed seven case studies that focused on understanding the relationship between the genetic characteristics of populations and landscape features. The first five studies focus on connectivity within linear networks (i.e., connected stream and lakes), whereas the latter two focus on systems that also involve terrestrial dispersal pathways. These studies provide strong evidence that landscape features affect genetic characteristics. Furthermore, the case studies present different methodologies to understand the relationship of landscape patterns and the genetics of populations.

Principal Component Analysis in R: An Examination of the Different Functions and Methods

Gregory B. Anderson

Principal component analysis (PCA) is a multivariate procedure aimed at reducing the dimensionality of multivariate data while accounting for as much of the variation in the original data set as possible. In R there are two general methods to perform PCA without any missing values: (1) spectral decomposition and (2) singular value decomposition. Both of these methods can be performed longhand using the functions eigen and svd, respectively, or can be performed using the many PCA functions found in the stats package and other additional available packages. This document focuses on comparing the different methods to perform PCA in R and provides appropriate visualization techniques to examine normality within the statistical package. More specifically this document compares six different functions either created for or can be used for PCA: eigen, princomp, svd, prcomp, PCA, and pca. Additionally, the results from the functions are compared using simulation procedure to see if the different methods differ in the eigenvalues, eigenvectors, and scores provided from the output.

Correlates of Sleep in Mammals: A model Building Approach to the Exploration of Ecological and Physiological Characteristics

Gregory B. Anderson

All mammals participate in some form of sleep; however, the amount of sleep a species receives varies widely among species. There can be big differences between even closely related species. Many comparative studies have sought to find parameters possibly associated with the observed variation in species using different attributes of sleep (e.g., slow wave sleep and active sleep), but less research has focused on the effects of variables on total sleep (i.e., slow wave + active sleep). Therefore, using a data set previously compiled by Allison and Circchetti (1976), I sought to identify possible ecological and constitutional correlates of total sleep in 59 mammal species. I used three constitutional variables and three ecological variables in an additive linear formulation using multiple least-squares regression.
